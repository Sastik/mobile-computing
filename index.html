1. Explain about Global System of mobile communication?

Mobile Communication is the use of technology that allows us to communicate with others in different locations without the use of any physical connection.

GSM stands for Global System for Mobile Communication. It is an open and digital cellular technology used for mobile communication.

Features:

  • Supports international roaming: GSM networks allow users to roam internationally and still maintain connectivity.
  • Clear voice clarity: GSM ensures clear voice transmission for high-quality communication.
  • Ability to support multiple handheld devices: GSM networks can accommodate multiple handheld devices simultaneously.

Services:

  • Data services: GSM specifies different mechanisms for data transmission, allowing for data rates of up to 9600 bits/s.
  • Tele services: Tele services include video calls and short text messages (SMS).
  • Supplementary services: Supplementary services offer advanced features such as conference calls, call waiting, and call forwarding.
  • GSM security: GSM offers several security features using confidential information stored in the individual SIM, including authentication, encryption, and confidentiality.

Advantages:

  • Compatibility: GSM is widely used around the world, so it is compatible with many different networks and devices.
  • Security: GSM offers enhanced security features such as authentication, encryption, and confidentiality, which helps to protect the userâ€™s privacy and data.
  • Cost effectiveness: In wireless communication, there is no need for any physical infrastructure. Hence, the cost is reduced.
  • Speed: The network connectivity or the accessibility was much improved in accuracy and speed.
  • Constant connectivity: Constant connectivity ensures that people can respond to emergencies relatively quickly.

Disadvantages:

  • Limited coverage: GSM networks may have limited coverage in some remote areas, which can make it difficult for users to make calls or access the internet.
  • Network congestion: GSM networks may become congested during peak hours, which can lead to dropped calls or poor call quality.
  • Data transfer speed: GSM networks offer relatively slow data transfer speeds compared to newer technologies such as 3G and 4G.
  • Limited capacity: GSM networks have a limited capacity for handling large volumes of data, which can be a disadvantage for users who require high-speed internet access or other data-intensive applications.

3. Management of Mobility in GSM Network, write down the functionality of HLR & VLR

Mobility is a process that allows transferring the mobile service as the mobile station moves from one cell to another.

Mobility management in GSM networks has the purpose of monitoring subscribers and their location to enable them to receive voice calls, SMSs, or data services.

Key Components and Processes:

  • Location Management: The process of tracking the current location of mobile devices within the network.
  • Roaming: The ability for a cellular customer to automatically make and receive voice calls, send and receive data, or access other services when travelling outside the geographical coverage area of the home network.
  • Handover Management: The process of transferring an ongoing call or data session from one cell to another as a mobile device moves between cells.

GSM networks use various handover techniques, including:

  • Intra-cell handover
  • Inter-cell handover
  • Inter-BSC handover
  • Inter-MSC handover

Function of HLR (Home Location Register):

  • Subscriber Profile Management: The HLR maintains detailed subscriber profiles containing information such as the subscriber's mobile number, authentication information, service subscription details.
  • Call Routing: It provides call routing and roaming facilities by combining with MSC and VLR.
  • Authentication: The HLR stores authentication information used to verify the identity of subscribers when they attempt to access network services or make calls.
  • Subscriber Information Storage: The HLR stores permanent data about subscribers, including their service profiles.

Function of VLR (Visitor Location Register):

  • Temporary Subscriber Data Storage: When a mobile device enters a new location area and performs a location update, the VLR stores temporary information about the subscriber.
  • Call Routing Optimization: The VLR optimizes call routing by storing subscriber information locally, reducing the need to query the HLR for every call or message.
  • Handover Support: During handover procedures, the VLR assists in transferring subscriber information between cells or location areas to ensure uninterrupted service as mobile devices move between coverage areas.
  • Service Management: The VLR handles service provision and restrictions as directed by the HLR and manages supplementary services for the subscriber.

4. What is Hand-off? Differentiate between Soft Hand-off and Hard Hand-off

Hand-off (or Handover) is a process in mobile communication systems where an ongoing call or data session is transferred from one cell to another without disconnecting the session.

  • This ensures seamless communication as a mobile user moves through different coverage areas.
  • The primary goal of a hand-off is to maintain uninterrupted service.

Types of Handoffs:

  • Hard Handoff: In this strategy, the connection to the current cell is broken before a new connection is established with the target cell.
  • Soft Handoff: Soft Handoff is a mechanism in which the device gets connected with two or more base stations at the same time.
  • Delayed Handoff: Delayed handoff occurs when no base station is available for accepting the transfer. The call continues until the signal strength reaches a threshold, and after that, the call is dropped.
  • Mobile-Assisted Handoff: Mobile-Assisted handoff is generally used when a mobile phone helps a base station to transfer the call to another base station with better-improvised connectivity and more signal strength.

    • Public Wi-Fi networks can be less secure, making it easier for hackers to intercept data.
    • Mobile devices can be more vulnerable to malware and phishing attacks.

6. What is Multiple Access Technique? Do a Comparative Study among FDMA, TDMA & CDMA?

Multiple Access Technique refers to a method that allows multiple users to share the same frequency communication channel effectively without interference.

  • The multiplexing is provided in the physical layer, and the shared communication medium can be wired cables or wireless spectrum.

Types of Multiple Access Techniques:

1. Frequency Division Multiple Access (FDMA):

The available frequency band is divided into multiple frequency channels, each assigned to a different user or communication link.

  • Usage: Commonly used in analog systems and early cellular networks.
  • Advantages: Simple implementation, minimal timing requirements.
  • Disadvantages: Inefficient use of bandwidth, fixed allocation of frequencies can lead to underutilization.

2. Time Division Multiple Access (TDMA):

The available frequency is divided into time slots, and each user is assigned a specific time slot during which they can transmit or receive data.

  • Usage: Used in digital systems such as GSM.
  • Advantages: Dynamic allocation of time slots.
  • Disadvantages: Requires potential delay due to time slot allocation.

3. Code Division Multiple Access (CDMA):

Each user is assigned a unique code, and all users can transmit simultaneously over the same frequency band.

  • Usage: Used in 3G networks and technologies.
  • Advantages: High capacity, better resistance to interference.
  • Disadvantages: Complex implementation, requires sophisticated signal processing.

8. Define Packet Switching and Circuit Switching

Packet Switching:

Packet switching is a communication method where data is divided into smaller units called packets and transmitted over the network.

  • Each packet contains the source and destination addresses, as well as other information needed for routing.

Advantages of Packet Switching:

  • Efficient use of bandwidth: Packet switching is efficient because bandwidth is shared among multiple users.
  • Flexible: Packet switching is flexible and can handle a wide range of data rates and packet sizes.
  • Scalable: Packet switching is highly scalable and can handle large amounts of traffic on a network.
  • Lower cost: Packet switching is less expensive than circuit switching.

Disadvantages of Packet Switching:

  • Higher latency: Packet switching has higher latency.
  • Costs: Installation costs of packet switching are expensive.
  • Packet loss: Packet switching can result in packet loss due to congestion on the network.

Examples:

The Internet, where data such as emails, web pages, and files are transmitted using protocols like TCP/IP.

Circuit Switching:

Circuit switching is defined as the method of switching which is used for establishing a dedicated communication path between the sender and the receiver. The link which is established between the sender and the receiver is in the physical form.

  • This path remains reserved exclusively for the session until it is terminated.

Advantages of Circuit Switching:

  • Fixed Bandwidth: The bandwidth used is fixed.
  • Fixed Data Transmission Rate: The rate at which the data is transmitted is fixed.
  • No Waiting Time During Switching: While switching, no time is wasted in waiting.

Disadvantages of Circuit Switching:

  • High Bandwidth Requirement: Since dedicated channels are used, the bandwidth required is more.
  • Inefficient Resource Utilization: The utilization of resources is not full.
  • High Cost: Circuit switching is expensive.

Examples:

Traditional telephone networks and ISDN where a continuous, dedicated connection is required.

9. What is GPRS? Define the Protocols of a GPRS System

GPRS stands for General Packet Radio Service. It is the modified version of GSM architecture. GPRS is a packet-oriented mobile data mechanism that can carry data packets as well.

  • GPRS is able to transmit voice as well as data packets.
  • GPRS provides moderate-speed data transfer, allowing users to access services such as email, web browsing, and multimedia messaging.

GPRS Network Architecture:

GPRS tries to make maximum use of the existing physical structure of GSM. It has introduced a new entity named GPRS support nodes (GSN) whose responsibility is to route and deliver a data packet.

  • Mobile Station (MS): Requires enhanced mobile stations to support high-speed data packets.
  • Base Station Controller (BSC): In GSM architecture there is one component called BSC. But in GPRS there is one component is added to BSC called PCU. PCU stands for Packet Control Unit. If the signal comes to BSC and that signal contains data, then PCU routes to the SGSN.
  • GPRS Support Nodes: GPRS support nodes are of two types:
    • Serving GPRS Support Node (SGSN): It is responsible for packet delivery, mobility management, and LLC (Logical Link Control) management.
    • Gateway GPRS Support Node (GGSN): It is responsible for acting as a mediator between the GPRS backbone and external data networks.
  • Internal Backbone Network: It is an IP-based network that is used to support the working of GPRS and this is responsible to carry new packets between different GSNs.
  • Mobility Support:
    • Attachment Procedure: Ensures that a mobile station can attach to the GPRS network.
    • Location and Handoff Management: Manages the location of the mobile station and handles the handoff process when the mobile station moves from one cell to another.
  • Routing Area: This is similar to the location area in GSM. The only difference is routing areas use fewer cells as routing areas are smaller than the location area.

10. Write a short note on a) Mobile IP b) IEEE 802.11 standard c) WLANs

a) Mobile IP

Mobile IP is a communication protocol that allows the users to move from one network to another with the same IP address. It ensures that the communication will continue without the userâ€™s sessions or connections being dropped.

  • Mobile Node (MN): The hand-held communication device that the user carries, e.g., Cell phone.
  • Home Agent (HA): A router in the home network to which the mobile node was originally connected.
  • Home Address: The permanent IP address assigned to the mobile node (within its home network).
  • Foreign Agent (FA): A router in a foreign network to which the mobile node is currently connected.

Advantages of Mobile IP:

  • Seamless Connectivity: Allows mobile devices to maintain ongoing sessions while moving across different networks.
  • Transparency: The communication with the correspondent node is transparent, meaning the correspondent node does not need to know the mobile node's current location.
  • Scalability: Can support a large number of mobile nodes and a variety of applications.

Challenges:

  • Security: Ensuring secure registration and data transmission is complex and critical.

b) IEEE 802.11 Standard

IEEE 802.11 standard defines the protocols for implementing wireless networking, ensuring interoperability between various wireless devices.

  • Stations (STA): All devices and equipment that are connected to the wireless LAN.
  • Each station has a wireless network interface controller.
  • Basic Service Set (BSS): A group of stations communicating at the physical layer level.

Advantages:

  • Flexible Architecture: Supports both temporary smaller networks and larger, more permanent ones.
  • Prolonged Battery Life: Efficient power-saving protocols extend mobile device battery life without compromising network connections.

Disadvantages:

  • Noisy Channels: Due to reliance on radio waves, signals may experience interference from nearby devices.
  • Speed: Generally, WLANs offer slower speeds compared to wired LANs.

Applications:

  • Home Networking: Wireless access for computers, smartphones, and smart home devices.
  • Enterprise Networking: Provides connectivity in office environments, supporting a wide range of devices and applications.
  • Public Wi-Fi: Offered in public places such as cafes, airports, and hotels to provide internet access to customers.

Challenges:

  • Security: Wireless networks are more susceptible to security threats.
  • Range and Coverage: The effective range of wireless networks is limited, and signal strength can degrade over distance and obstacles.

c) WLANs

WLANs are networks that enable devices to connect and communicate wirelessly within a limited area, such as a home, office, or campus. WLANs utilize radio frequency (RF) technology to transmit data, providing mobility and flexibility to users.

  • Access Points (APs): Hubs that broadcast wireless signals and manage traffic, allowing wireless devices to connect to a wired network.
  • Wireless Clients: Devices like laptops, smartphones, tablets, and IoT gadgets that connect to the network via APs.

Advantages:

  • Mobility: Users can move freely within the coverage area without losing network connectivity, making WLANs ideal for dynamic and mobile environments.
  • Ease of Installation: Wireless networks eliminate the need for extensive cabling, simplifying installation and reducing costs.
  • Scalability: WLANs can be easily expanded by adding more APs, accommodating growing numbers of devices and users.

Applications:

  • Home Networking: Provides wireless internet access for various household devices, including computers, smartphones, smart TVs, and IoT devices.
  • Enterprise Networking: Supports a wide range of business applications, allowing employees to connect laptops, tablets, and other devices to the corporate network.
  • Educational Institutions: Enables students and staff to access online resources, collaborate, and communicate wirelessly across campus.
  • Public Wi-Fi: Offers internet access in public spaces such as cafes, airports, hotels, and libraries.

Disadvantages:

  • WLAN requires license.
  • Limited Area: Coverage is typically restricted to a specific area.
  • Interference: Communication may be disrupted by environmental factors such as rain or thunder.
  • Security: Wireless networks are more vulnerable to attacks, making transmitted data susceptible to interception.

11. What do you mean by tunnelling and encapsulation in the context of Mobile IP?

Tunnelling:

Tunneling establishes a virtual path (tunnel) for data packets between two points (entry and endpoint).

  • Process: Packets enter the tunnel at the entry point, travel through it, and exit unchanged at the endpoint.
  • Purpose: Ensures seamless delivery of packets to the mobile node (MN) as it moves across networks.

Encapsulation:

The process of wrapping an original packet within a new packet.

  • Mechanism:
    • The home agent (HA) takes the original packet, inserts it into the data part of a new packet, and adds a new IP header (outer header).
    • The outer header routes the packet to the mobile node's care-of address (CoA).
  • Decapsulation: The reverse process where the encapsulated packet is extracted from the outer packet.

14. What is WAP Gateway? Write down its functionalities.

A WAP (Wireless Application Protocol) Gateway serves as a crucial intermediary in mobile communication networks.

  • Protocol Conversion: Translating requests and responses between WAP protocols used by mobile devices and standard internet protocols like HTTP or HTTPS.
  • Content Adaptation: Modifying web content to suit mobile device constraints, such as converting HTML to Wireless Markup Language (WML) or resizing images for smaller screens.
  • Security Enhancement: Providing encryption and authentication features to secure data transmitted between mobile devices and web servers over wireless networks.
  • Compression: Optimizing data transmission by compressing web content before delivery to reduce bandwidth usage and improve loading times.
  • Caching: Storing frequently accessed content to reduce latency and enhance performance for mobile users.

Disadvantages:

  • Performance Limitations: WAP Gateways can reduce the overall performance of mobile internet access due to the additional processing required for protocol conversion.
  • Dependency on Service Providers: Mobile users are reliant on service providers to maintain and operate WAP Gateways.

25. Explain Personal Communications Services (PCS) architecture.

Personal Communications Services (PCS) refer to a set of wireless communication services that provide voice and data transmission to individual users.

Architecture:

  1. Mobile Stations (MS): Mobile stations, also known as user equipment or mobile devices, are the handheld devices used by subscribers to access PCS services. These devices can include smartphones, feature phones, tablets, and other wireless-enabled gadgets.
  2. Base Stations (BS): Base stations, also called cell sites or radio towers, transmit and receive signals to and from mobile stations, providing connectivity between users and the network.
  3. Mobile Switching Center (MSC): It connects mobile users to the Public Switched Telephone Network (PSTN) or other mobile networks, facilitating voice and data communication between subscribers.
  4. Home Location Register (HLR) and Visitor Location Register (VLR):
    • The Home Location Register (HLR) is a database that stores subscriber information and authentication data for PCS users.
    • The Visitor Location Register (VLR) is a temporary database that stores subscriber information for users currently within the coverage area of a particular MSC.
  5. Authentication Center (AuC): The Authentication Center is responsible for authenticating PCS subscribers and ensuring the security of communication by generating and verifying authentication keys.

26. What is Network signaling?

Network signaling is the process used in telecommunications networks to establish, control, and terminate connections between devices. It involves the exchange of control information, separate from the actual data being transmitted, to manage network operations and ensure efficient communication.

Features:

  • Control Information: Transmits instructions for call setup, routing, and termination.
  • Separate Channels: Often uses separate channels from data transmission for reliability and security.
  • Network Management: Manages network resources, including bandwidth allocation and error detection.
  • Session Control: Handles the establishment, maintenance, and termination of communication sessions.

Types of Signaling:

  1. In-band Signaling: Uses the same channel as the voice or data to send control information.
  2. Out-of-band Signaling: Uses separate channels for control information, like Signaling System 7 (SS7) in telephone networks.
  3. Common Channel Signaling (CCS): A type of out-of-band signaling where a single dedicated channel carries signaling information for multiple data channels.

Applications:

  • Telephone Networks: Manages call setup, routing, and teardown in traditional and mobile phone systems.
  • Internet Protocol (IP) Networks: Facilitates voice over IP (VoIP) calls and multimedia sessions.
  • Cellular Networks: Controls mobile communication processes such as handovers and roaming.

28. Explain International Mobile Telecommunications 2000 (IMT 2000) vision or 3G Mobile Telecommunication

International Mobile Telecommunications 2000 (IMT-2000) is a global standard set by the International Telecommunication Union (ITU) for third-generation (3G) wireless communications. 3G (Third Generation) mobile telecommunication is an advanced cellular network technology that offers faster data transmission, enabling users to access the internet, multimedia content, and advanced communication services on their mobile devices.

Advantages:

  • a) High-Speed Data Transmission: Offers data transfer rates exceeding 0.2 Mbps, allowing for faster internet browsing, downloading, and streaming.

  • c) Multimedia Access: Enables advanced multimedia functionalities such as music streaming, video calling, and television services on mobile devices.
  • d) Enhanced Connectivity: Facilitates seamless connectivity and communication, supporting a wide range of applications and services.

Applications:

  1. Enhanced Mobile Communication: Improved voice calls, text messaging, and internet access.
  2. Video Conferencing: Enables mobile video calls and conferencing.
  3. Mobile Internet: Provides faster and more reliable mobile internet connectivity.
  4. Multimedia Messaging: Supports sending and receiving multimedia messages, including images and videos.
  5. Mobile TV and Streaming: Facilitates mobile television and video streaming services.
